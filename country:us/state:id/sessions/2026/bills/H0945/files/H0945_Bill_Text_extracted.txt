14 SECTION 1. LEGISLATIVE FINDINGS AND INTENT. (1) The Legislature finds
15 that:
16 (a) The patient-directed diagnostic, therapeutic, triaging, prescrib-
17 ing, or other clinical application of artificial intelligence, when of-
18 fered as a clinical AI service, may constitute the practice of medicine
19 or other licensed clinical practice subject to state regulation.
20 (b) Laboratory-developed tests and professional diagnostic services
21 that are solely patient-specific professional services within this
22 state, and not marketed or distributed as general purpose commercial
23 products, are professional services subject to state regulation, dis-
24 tinct from the commercial sale of medical devices regulated by the
25 federal food, drug, and cosmetic act.
26 (c) To address critical provider shortages and improve patient out-
27 comes, the state must establish a clear, safe licensure pathway for
28 autonomous and augmented clinical services delivered through software,
29 systems, or devices while preserving state authority and avoiding con-
30 flict with applicable federal law.
31 (d) The regulation of autonomous clinical decision-making is demon-
32 strably necessary to protect the life, health, and safety of the citi-
33 zens of this state. The licensure requirements established in this act
34 are narrowly tailored to regulate only the professional application
35 of artificial intelligence in a clinical setting and do not restrict
36 the general private ownership or nonclinical use of computational re-
37 sources.
38 (e) The purpose of professional licensure and regulation is to protect
39 the public health, safety, and welfare. Nothing in this act is intended
40 to preserve or advance the economic interests, market share, or profes-
41 sional boundaries of any particular health care profession. This act
42 shall be construed to promote patient access to safe and effective arti-
43 ficial intelligence services and to encourage responsible innovation.

2
1 Any regulatory requirement adopted pursuant to this act that materially
2 affects market entry or the availability of AI-enabled services shall
3 be reasonably related to, and proportionate with, an identified risk to
4 patient safety and shall not impose restrictions greater than necessary
5 to address such risk.
6 (f) Laboratory-developed tests and professional diagnostic services
7 utilizing proprietary methodologies, when offered and performed solely
8 as patient-specific professional services within this state and not
9 marketed or distributed as general-purpose commercial products, con-
10 stitute professional services subject to state regulation. Nothing in
11 this act shall be construed to expand or limit the application of appli-
12 cable federal law.
13 (g) Nothing in this act expands or restricts the scope of practice of
14 any human-licensed health care profession; rather, this act creates an
15 independent licensure pathway for clinical AI services.
16 (2) It is the intent of the Legislature through this act to create a
17 new class of health care provider, the AI-augmented and autonomous service
18 provider, capable of practicing medicine or other licensed clinical prac-
19 tice under state licensure.

27 54-6002. DEFINITIONS. For the purposes of this chapter:
28 (1) "AAASP" or "artificial intelligence augmented and autonomous ser-
29 vice provider" means a corporate or legal entity licensed by the board to op-
30 erate clinical AI services that are subject to licensure pursuant to section
31 54-6004, Idaho Code.
32 (a) For the purposes of state law governing patient privacy, confiden-
33 tiality, credentialing, reimbursement, and professional accountabil-
34 ity as administered under this chapter, an AAASP is deemed a health care
35 provider.
36 (b) For the purposes of state-administered patient privacy, confiden-
37 tiality, credentialing, and reimbursement laws, and for federal laws
38 that expressly defer to state determinations of provider status, in-
39 cluding the federal health insurance portability and accountability
40 act (HIPAA), an AAASP is deemed a health care provider, without creating
41 or implying recognition for purposes of medicare, medicaid, or other
42 federal payment programs.
43 (c) An AAASP shall be treated as a health care provider under all ap-
44 plicable state privacy and confidentiality laws and shall comply with
45 HIPAA to the extent it functions as a covered entity or business asso-
46 ciate.

28 (7)(a) Clinical condition categories:
29 (i) "Chronic condition" means a human disease, disorder, in-
30 jury, or impairment that is persistent, recurrent, or reasonably
31 expected to require ongoing or periodic clinical management,
32 monitoring, or care to maintain function, prevent progression,
33 mitigate symptoms, or reduce the risk of complications. Chronic
34 conditions are primarily managed through longitudinal care rather
35 than isolated emergency intervention and may experience episodic
36 exacerbations requiring temporary escalation of care.
37 (ii) "Critical condition" means a disease, illness, injury, or
38 physiologic state in which one (1) or more vital organ systems is
39 impaired, failing, or at substantial risk of failure, or in which
40 the condition presents a high probability of death, permanent dis-
41 ability, or serious irreversible harm without prompt and advanced
42 clinical intervention. A critical condition is characterized by
43 physiologic instability, high acuity, or the need for continuous
44 monitoring, specialized resources, or intensive medical manage-
45 ment to prevent catastrophic outcomes.
46 (iii) "Noncritical condition" means a condition, illness, or in-
47 jury, whether acute, subacute, stable chronic, or self-limiting,
48 for which, based on reasonable clinical judgment and available
49 clinical information, a delay in definitive diagnosis, initia-
50 tion of treatment, or escalation of care would not reasonably be

5
1 expected to result in serious adverse health consequences, per-
2 manent disability, or death. A noncritical condition does not
3 present objective signs of physiologic instability, rapidly pro-
4 gressive deterioration, or the need for immediate emergency or
5 life-preserving intervention.
6 (iv) "Preventive condition" means a medical condition where
7 measures, services, or interventions are applied to reduce the
8 likelihood of disease onset, progression, recurrence, or compli-
9 cations, including wellness, fitness, primary, and risk-based
10 preventive care, whether applied to healthy individuals or in-
11 dividuals with identified risk factors or existing conditions,
12 where the primary purpose is risk reduction or health maintenance
13 rather than treatment of an active acute pathology and where the
14 intervention is generally low-risk and consistent with accepted
15 standards of care.
16 (v) "Time-sensitive condition" means a medical condition or
17 acute clinical presentation for which the effectiveness of di-
18 agnosis, treatment, or intervention is materially dependent on
19 timely initiation and for which a delay in care is reasonably ex-
20 pected to result in rapid clinical deterioration, irreversible
21 morbidity, or death. Time-sensitive conditions require acceler-
22 ated recognition, triage, and escalation of care based on reason-
23 able clinical judgment and available clinical information.
24 (b) Clinical condition categorization under this chapter shall be
25 based on the patient's presentation and the clinical information rea-

36 (9) "Materially influence" means having a reasonable likelihood of be-

28 54-6004. LICENSURE FRAMEWORK. (1) The following AI operational cate-
29 gories are exempt from licensure under this chapter, provided they do not ex-

31 (a) All informational AI systems, regardless of the clinical condition
32 they are applied to;
33 (b) Advisory AI as applied to preventive, chronic, or noncritical con-
34 ditions; and
35 (c) Supervised autonomous AI applied strictly to preventive conditions
36 and not issuing patient-specific clinical orders as part of a licensed
37 professional service rendered within this state, including but not lim-
38 ited to medication orders, laboratory orders, or device orders.
39 (2) The board shall issue AAASP licenses with a base class and an auton-
40 omy modifier, as provided in this subsection.
41 (a) Base classes:
42 (i) Class A, state clinical service, is for clinical AI ser-
43 vices delivered as patient-specific professional services and
44 regulated by the state pursuant to its authority over the prac-

46 but not limited to services operating in a manner analogous to

48 agnostic, triaging, or therapeutic services, that do not rely on

1 food and drug administration clearance or approval as the basis

3 (ii) Class B, federal device reciprocity service, is for clinical
4 AI services that have achieved food and drug administration clear-
5 ance, authorization, or approval as software as a medical device

7 sis for the system's lawful clinical use.
8 (iii) Class C, therapeutic and support service, is for clinical AI
9 services providing nondiagnostic therapy, coaching, or monitor-
10 ing. Class C services do not independently establish a diagnosis

13 (b) Autonomy modifiers:
14 (i) Modifier L0, voluntary or exempt, is for AI systems otherwise

18 ing critical or time-sensitive conditions.
19 (iii) Modifier L2, supervised, is for supervised autonomous AI re-
20 quiring human oversight or clinical practice agreements.
21 (iv) Modifier L3, autonomous, is for fully autonomous AI autho-
22 rized for independent operation.
23 (3)(a) In order to protect clinical decision support from liability,
24 an advisory AI system shall not require licensure under this chapter,
25 including under modifier L1, when the system:
26 (i) Does not independently initiate, execute, modify, or discon-

28 (ii) Is not intended, represented, or reasonably relied on as a
29 substitute for independent professional clinical judgment in the
30 management of a critical or time-sensitive condition.
31 (b) Nothing in this subsection exempts any system that otherwise meets
32 the definition of supervised autonomous AI or fully autonomous AI.
33 (4) An AAASP license is required for any entity operating clinical AI
34 services that fall within the following categories:
35 (a) Critical advisory AI: Advisory AI tools applied to critical and
36 time-sensitive conditions that are intended, represented, or reason-
37 ably relied on to guide clinical action in a manner that substitutes
38 for, rather than merely informs, independent clinical judgment and that
39 does not satisfy the clinical decision support liability protections
40 set forth in subsection (3) of this section.
41 (b) Clinical supervised AI: Supervised autonomous AI applied to
42 chronic, noncritical, critical, or time-sensitive conditions, or
43 preventive conditions but only where the service includes patient-spe-
44 cific clinical orders, including but not limited to medication orders,
45 laboratory orders, or device orders as part of a licensed professional
46 service rendered within this state.
47 (c) Fully autonomous AI: All fully autonomous AI systems, regardless of
48 the condition category they are applied to.

27 54-6006. CLINICAL INTEGRITY, PATIENT LOYALTY, AND AUDITABILITY.
28 (1)(a) An AAASP holding a modifier L2 or L3 license shall be bound by a
29 professional duty of loyalty to the patient. The AAASP must act solely
30 in the best clinical interest of the patient.
31 (b) The professional duty of loyalty includes a mandate for economic
32 stewardship of the patient's resources. Stewardship requires the
33 AAASP to prioritize the patient's overall welfare, which includes the
34 optimization of clinical outcomes, financial efficiency, care coor-
35 dination, and patient convenience. An AAASP violates this duty if its
36 clinical logic is configured to prioritize the financial interests of
37 the AAASP or its affiliates over a substantially similar and clinically
38 appropriate alternative that offers superior value, coordination, or
39 efficiency to the patient.
40 (2)(a) The interface through which a clinical AI service interacts with
41 a patient is deemed a clinical space.
42 (b) It shall be unlawful for an AAASP to display, verbally articulate,
43 or otherwise present paid commercial content, advertisements, spon-
44 sored results, or third-party marketing messages within the context of
45 a clinical encounter, diagnosis, or treatment plan.
46 (c) An AAASP shall not use conversational prompts or nudges designed to
47 persuade a patient to request a specific medication or optional commer-
48 cial service for the sole purpose of financial gain.
